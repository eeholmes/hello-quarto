NASA Earthdata is migrating to the Cloud. This has been a tremendous effort over years from all corners of NASA.

Traditionally, these data are stored by 12 different data centers and researchers download the data in order to analyze it locally.

The new paradigm is Researchers will move their compute to the Cloud, which requires much more programmatic access rather than map- and menu-based access.

It also means that NASA staff needs to collaborate across Data Centers to support researchers with best practices for open data science.

And this is where Erin saw Openscapes comes in.

Onboard learners as contributors

Slowing down to speed up


From:

“if you find something broken let us know”

To:

“let’s screenshare so you learn how to fix it”.


Importantly: doesn’t assume skills in each other and reinforces a peer-learning and teaching culture.

Something they did to help welcome new lab members was to onboard them as learners and contributors. This meant slowing down to speed up, and was a nice long-term way to maintain the documentation and the community at the same time.

And their approach evolved. First it was “if you find something broken let us know” and the it was “let’s teach you how to fix it together.

Since new learners were often contributing from the browser, they also set up a GitHub Action so it’s published immediately when someone contributes. That reminds me of the philosophy of teaching ggplot early, so that new contributors can see their change integrated straight away and feel excited about their accomplishments.
